The math under the hood

Every node is a tiny function. The start node hands you a number; each gate you wire it through transforms it — ×2, +5, −3, or ± (flip the sign). Wiring gates in a row is literally function composition: the output of one becomes the input of the next. Your job is to find an ordering whose composed result equals the target.

Because gates can be picked in any order and you have a limited move budget, you're searching a tree of possible chains for one that lands exactly on the target — the same shape of problem a pathfinding or graph-search algorithm solves. Solve a level and you've hand-run a tiny search.

The puzzles are never hard-coded. Each level is generated by working backward: the game starts from your input, applies a random valid chain of operations forward, and records the result as the target. Because that chain provably exists on the board, every level is guaranteed solvable — and the space of chains is effectively infinite.
